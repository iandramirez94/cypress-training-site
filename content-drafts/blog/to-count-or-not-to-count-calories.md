# To Count or Not to Count (Calories)

**By Ian Ramirez, Cypress Training Center**

Here's the question I get asked more than almost anything else: "Do I need to count calories?"

The answer is yes. And also no.

Let me explain.

---

## The Problem with the Calorie Counting Debate

The nutrition world loves to argue about calorie counting. One side says it's the only thing that matters. The other side says it's obsessive and unnecessary.

They're both right. And they're both wrong.

Here's what I tell my clients: **you need to count calories for 1-2 weeks. Just once. And then you're done.**

---

## Think of It as Calibration

If I handed you a barbell and asked you to load 185 pounds without looking at the plates, could you do it?

Probably not.

But if you loaded a barbell to 185 pounds a few times while actually checking the weight, you'd start to get a feel for it. Eventually, you could eyeball it pretty accurately.

**That's what tracking calories for 1-2 weeks does.** It calibrates your internal sense of portion sizes, calorie density, and where your food is actually coming from.

Most people are shockingly bad at estimating calories. Studies show we underestimate by 30-50% on average. That's not because we're dumb — it's because we've never actually measured.

---

## What You Learn in 1-2 Weeks of Tracking

When you meticulously track everything you eat for a week or two, a few things happen:

### 1. You See Where Your Calories Actually Come From

You might think your biggest calorie source is dinner. Then you track and realize it's the handful of nuts you grab at 3 PM, the cream in your coffee, and the "small" dessert after dinner.

Awareness changes behavior.

### 2. You Learn What Portions Actually Look Like

4 ounces of chicken looks smaller than you think. A tablespoon of peanut butter is way less than you've been using. A serving of rice is probably half of what you've been eating.

Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

### 3. You Get Better at Guesstimating

After a week of weighing and measuring, you develop an eye for it. You can look at a plate and think, "That's probably 500-600 calories." You won't be perfect, but you'll be close enough.

### 4. You Understand Your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)

TDEE is just a fancy term for **how many calories your body burns in a day**. It includes:
- Your metabolism (what you burn just existing)
- Daily movement (walking, fidgeting, etc.)
- Exercise (your workouts)

Most people have no clue what their TDEE actually is. Tracking for 1-2 weeks gives you a baseline. If you're maintaining your weight at 2,200 calories per day, congratulations — that's roughly your TDEE.

If you want to lose fat, eat a bit less. If you want to build muscle, eat a bit more. That's it.

---

## Why I Don't Recommend Counting Calories Forever

Because it's exhausting. And it's not necessary.

Once you've calibrated, you don't need to weigh every gram of food for the rest of your life. That's not sustainable, and frankly, it takes the joy out of eating.

**What you DO need is awareness.**

You now know:
- What a normal portion looks like
- Which foods are calorie-dense (and which aren't)
- Roughly how much you're eating on a typical day

That's enough.

---

## The Better Approach: Adjust Based on Results

Here's what I do with clients after the calibration phase:

**Eat intuitively, but pay attention to results.**

If your goal is fat loss:
- Eat mindfully (no tracking required)
- Weigh yourself weekly
- If you're losing 0.5-1 lb per week → keep doing what you're doing
- If you're not losing → cut back slightly (smaller portions, fewer snacks)
- If you're losing too fast (2+ lbs/week) → add a bit more food

If your goal is muscle gain:
- Eat enough to fuel your workouts
- Track your lifts (are you getting stronger?)
- If you're gaining 0.5-1 lb per week and getting stronger → keep going
- If you're not gaining → eat more
- If you're gaining too fast (2+ lbs/week) → dial it back

**The scale and your performance tell you everything you need to know.** You don't need to count calories to know if you're on track.

---

## When to Track Again

There are a few times when it makes sense to go back to tracking for a week or two:

1. **You've plateaued.** If fat loss stalls or muscle gain stops, a week of tracking can show you where things drifted.
2. **You're prepping for something specific.** Competition, photoshoot, vacation — fine, get precise for a few weeks.
3. **You're way off your goals.** If you've been "eating clean" but gaining unwanted weight, tracking will show you the truth.

Otherwise? Don't bother.

---

## The Bottom Line

**Count calories for 1-2 weeks. Learn the lessons. Then stop.**

Use that calibration to eat with awareness, not obsession. Adjust based on how your body responds, not what an app tells you to do.

You don't need to track every bite for the rest of your life. You just need to know what you're doing — and whether it's working.
